⚡ Section 01 — Physics (Main Focus)

Electrical Power
& Light Intensity

Physics is the main focus of this project. By measuring voltage (V) and current (I) with a multimeter, we calculate electrical power (P = VI) for each lamp. This determines how much light energy each plant receives. We also calculate total energy (E = Pt) to compare efficiency.

⚡

Electrical Power: P = VI

Power (Watts) = Voltage × Current. We measured both with a multimeter for each lamp. Higher wattage → brighter lamp → more light energy reaching the plant.

🔌

Resistance & Brightness

Using Ohm's Law (V = IR), different resistor values reduce current and dim the lamp. We created Low / Medium / High light intensity groups this way.

🔋

Electrical Energy: E = Pt

Total energy consumed = Power × Time (seconds). We compared energy used per group against growth rate to analyse light efficiency at the end of the experiment.

P = VI
Power (W) = Voltage (V) × Current (A)
Group A: 30V × 0.1A = 3W  |  Group B: 7V × 1A = 7W  |  Group C: 10V × 1,2A = 12W
E = Pt
Energy (J) = Power (W) × Time (s)
Group A: 3W × (10 days × 24 hr × 3600 s) = 2,592,000 J  |  Group B: 7W × (10 days × 24 hr × 3600 s) = 6,048,000 J  |  Group C: 12W × (10 days × 24 hr × 3600 s) = 10,368,000 J
💡

Key Physics Finding

The experiment showed that electrical power follows the relationship P = VI, meaning power increases when either voltage or current increases. Lamps with higher power produced greater light intensity, which resulted in faster plant growth.

Group C (12W) provided the highest energy output and brightest light, leading to the most plant growth, while Group A (3W) had the lowest energy and weakest growth. This demonstrates that light intensity is directly related to electrical power and energy supplied over time (E = Pt).

Overall, higher electrical energy leads to increased light intensity, which enhances photosynthesis and promotes better plant growth.

🌿 Section 02 — Biology

Transpiration &
Plant Growth

Light intensity controls transpiration through stomatal opening. This affects water uptake, cell elongation, and ultimately plant height — explaining why different lamp powers produced different growth results.

💧

Transpiration

Water evaporates from mesophyll cell walls into air spaces, then diffuses through open stomata down a concentration gradient (high inside → low outside).

🌿

Light & Stomata

Guard cells use light energy to pump K⁺ in — water follows by osmosis, making them turgid and opening the pore. More light = wider stomata = faster transpiration.

📏

Water Uptake & Growth

Faster transpiration creates tension in xylem, pulling more water up from roots. Water is needed for photosynthesis, turgor pressure, and cell elongation — all increasing plant height.

🔗

The Light → Growth Chain

More light → stomata open wider → faster transpiration → more water via xylem → more photosynthesis + cell elongation → greater plant height. Write your explanation of how your results support this chain.

Key Biology Finding

Plants exposed to higher light intensity ( Group C ) showed slightly greater overall growth compared to lower light conditions. By Day 10, Group C reached about 25.50 cm, compared to 25.00 cm in Group B and 26.40 cm in Group A, showing that growth differences were present but not extremely large.

This suggests that while increased light intensity can enhance photosynthesis, there is a limit where additional light does not significantly increase growth. Other factors such as water, nutrients, or optimal light levels may also influence plant development.

Overall, the results support the idea that light intensity affects plant growth, but only up to an optimal range.

⚗️ Section 03 — Chemistry

Soil Chemistry &
Plant Metabolism

Chemistry underpins every process — from soil pH controlling nutrient availability, to NPK nutrients plants absorb, to the photosynthesis reaction converting light into glucose. These chemical processes explain why the right conditions are essential for growth.

🧪

Soil pH

We measured soil pH with a pHmeter. Most plants absorb nutrients best at pH 6–7. Below 6: toxic ions form. Above 7: phosphorus becomes insoluble. A constant pH of 7 was measured and maintained for all trials to ensure a fair test.

🌱

NPK Nutrients

Nitrogen drives leaf growth; Phosphorus supports ATP and roots; Potassium regulates stomatal opening. All three were supplied equally to avoid nutrition being a confounding variable through the regulated soil mixture provided in the lab.

⚗️

Photosynthesis Rate

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. Higher light intensity increases the rate of this reaction, producing more glucose for growth.

6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
Photosynthesis Equation
Light intensity affects how fast photosynthesis happens in plants.[1] When light intensity is low, it acts as a limiting factor, meaning it slows down the process because there isn't enough energy for the plant to make glucose.[2] As the lamp power increases, more light energy is provided, so the rate of photosynthesis increases and more glucose is produced.[3] However, after a certain point, increasing light intensity does not make much difference because other factors like carbon dioxide or temperature start to limit the reaction instead.[4]
⚗️

Key Chemistry Finding

This experiment investigated whether light intensity affects plant growth. The results a lack of a clear relationship, as the low power group (26.40 cm) grew slightly taller than the medium (25.00 cm) and high power groups (25.50 cm). Although higher light intensity should theoretically increase photosynthesis and growth, this was not observed, suggesting another factor may have limited growth. Limitations may include small sample size or nutrient limitation.

02

Fertiliser decomposition reactions

NPK fertilizers supply the essential nutrients nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium in forms plants can absorb as ions (NO₃⁻/NH₄⁺, PO₄³⁻, and K⁺). [1] Nitrogen is reduced into amino acids, enabling protein and chlorophyll synthesis, which increases photosynthesis and promotes leaf growth. [2] Phosphorus is taken up as phosphate and used to form ATP (ADP + Pi → ATP), providing energy for metabolic processes such as cell division and root development. [3] Potassium remains as K⁺ ions in solution, acting as an enzyme cofactor and regulating osmotic balance and stomatal function, which controls water movement and gas exchange. [4] Together, these nutrients enhance biochemical reactions, leading to faster growth and stronger plants. [5]

Relating light intensity to photosynthesis rate

📝 Conclusion


(1) Borderline: — This experiment investigated whether light intensity, controlled by electrical power (P = VI), affects plant growth measured by height. Based on our results, there was no clear or strong relationship between light intensity and plant growth. Although the highest power lamp was expected to produce the greatest growth, the data showed that low power actually had the tallest plants 26.40 cm, compared to medium power (25.00 cm) and high power (25.50 cm). This suggests that increasing light intensity did not significantly increase plant growth in this experiment.

(2) From results, — There is no clear correlation between light intensity with plant growth, as all three lamps with varying powers produced similar plant growths

(3) Linking to theory — Theory suggests that plant growth is directly correlated to light intensity, as the light energy provided the required activation energy for the photosynthesis reaction to produce glucose for plant growth

(4) Limitations — There were several limitations in this experiment. The sample size was small, with only two plants per group, which reduces reliability. Heat produced by the lamps may have affected plant growth by increasing evaporation or stressing the plants, as plant growth has visibily stunted at day 6 of the experiment. There may also have been inconsistencies in watering, measurement accuracy, or environmental conditions such as temperature and airflow.

(5) Improvements — To improve the experiment, future investigations could use LED lamps with known light intensity (lux) to remove heat as a variable. Increasing the number of plants per group would improve reliability, and controlling other variables such as temperature, water supply, and carbon dioxide levels would ensure a fairer test.
